What Are "Unblocked Games" Sites?

If you've spent any time trying to play games at school, you've probably encountered the term "unblocked games." It refers to a category of websites that host browser games on domain names that school content filters haven't flagged yet.

Here's how it works: schools use content filtering software — GoGuardian, Securly, Lightspeed, Cisco Umbrella, and others — that maintain databases of websites categorized by content type. Any domain tagged as "gaming" gets blocked automatically. Major platforms like Poki, CrazyGames, Miniclip, and Kongregate are all in these databases and get blocked on virtually every school network.

Unblocked games sites work around this by hosting games on domain names that look neutral or educational — names that the filter databases haven't catalogued yet. Because the filter doesn't recognize the domain as a gaming site, it doesn't block it. Students can access the games normally.

The most well-known unblocked games platforms include Unblocked Games 66, Unblocked Games 76, Unblocked Games WTF, and Unblocked Games 911. Each hosts hundreds of browser games, including Monkey Mart.

How School Filters Work — And Why This Site Gets Through

Understanding how school content filters work helps explain why some sites get blocked and others don't. The filters don't analyze the content of individual pages in real time — that would be too slow and computationally expensive. Instead, they use pre-built databases of domain names categorized by content type.

When you try to visit a website, the filter checks the domain against its database. If the domain is categorized as "gaming," "entertainment," or another blocked category, the request is denied. If the domain isn't in the database — or is categorized as something neutral — the request goes through.

Major gaming platforms like Poki and CrazyGames are in every filter database because they're well-known, high-traffic gaming sites that have been there for years. A dedicated fan site for a single game, with a domain name that doesn't obviously signal "gaming," is far less likely to be in those databases.

This isn't a guarantee — filters update regularly, and no site can promise it will always work on every school network. But in practice, dedicated fan sites like MonkeyMartHub work on school networks far more consistently than major gaming platforms.
